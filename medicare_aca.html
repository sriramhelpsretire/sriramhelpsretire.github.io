Medicare

What is Medicare? What are the components of Medicare?

Medicare is a Federal Health Insurance program in the U.S. primarily for individuals aged 65 or older, as well as younger people with specific conditions. It helps cover costs for hospital days, doctor visits, and preventative services through various parts.

Part A, B, C and D are the components. They do not cover all medical expenses and long term care.

Key Components of Medicare
Part A (Hospital Insurance): Covers inpatient hospital stays, care in a skilled nursing facility, hospice care, and some home health care.
Part B (Medical Insurance): Covers outpatient care, doctor services, medical supplies, and preventive services.
Part C (Medicare Advantage): Private insurance plans approved by Medicare that bundle Parts A and B, and often Part D, into one plan.
Part D (Prescription Drug Coverage): Private insurance plans that cover the cost of prescription drugs.
Eligibility and Enrollment
Age 65+: Most people 65 or older who are U.S. citizens or permanent residents are eligible.
Disability/Condition: Younger individuals with disabilities, End-Stage Renal Disease, or ALS may qualify.
Enrollment: You can choose between Original Medicare (Part A & B) or Medicare Advantage (Part C). Initial enrollment usually occurs around the 65th birthday to avoid penalties.
Costs
Part A: Usually premium-free if you or your spouse worked and paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years.
Part B: Involves a monthly premium, with higher costs for higher incomes.
Medigap: Private insurance, known as Medicare Supplement Insurance, can be purchased to help pay out-of-pocket costs in Original Medicare.

Medicare application should generally start during your 7-month Initial Enrollment Period (IEP), which begins 3 months before your 65th birthday month, includes that month, and ends 3 months after. For the earliest coverage, apply during the 3 months before your 65th birthday.
